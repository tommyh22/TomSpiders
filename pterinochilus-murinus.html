Pterinochilus murinus

The Orange Baboon Tarantula (OBT)

Introduction

Pterinochilus murinus, commonly referred to as the OBT (Orange Baboon Tarantula) or jokingly as the “Orange Bitey Thing,” is a beautiful and often misunderstood Old World species found across several Central African countries.

This species occurs in multiple colour forms, but the most commonly encountered in the hobby is the iconic bright orange morph.

Despite its reputation for being highly aggressive, this behaviour is often exaggerated and usually linked to incorrect husbandry rather than true temperament. Although very fast, the OBT is actually a shy and aloof species that prefers to hide away and avoid unnecessary interaction. In most situations, it will retreat rather than seek confrontation.

Interestingly, OBTs are known to display “semi-arboreal” tendencies, creating dense webbing higher up in the enclosure than typical terrestrial species. At the same time, they may also show fossorial (burrowing) behaviour — a fascinating combination seldom seen together.

This species thrives in arid to semi-arid conditions, similar to the dry habitats of its native regions.

Quick facts

  • Scientific name: Pterinochilus murinus
  • Common name: Orange Baboon Tarantula (OBT)
  • Endemic to: Central Africa
  • DLS: Approximately 6" (15 cm)
  • Growth rate: Fast
  • Life expectancy: Males 2–4 years, Females 10–15 years
  • Category: Old World
  • Defence mechanism: Strong venom, potentially defensive
  • Recommended experience: Advanced keepers only

Care Sheet

Temperature: 24–28°C

Humidity: 50–70% (good ventilation, avoid overly moist substrate)

Enclosure: Dry substrate, sturdy anchor points for webbing, cork bark hides

Feeding: Fast eater; offer appropriate-sized crickets/roaches regularly

Temperament

The OBT is often portrayed as aggressively defensive, but this is largely due to poor husbandry. In reality, it is a shy, secretive species that prefers to retreat rather than fight. However, it will defend itself if provoked, and keepers should always use caution.

No handling recommended.
